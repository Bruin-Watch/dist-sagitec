The Chief, The Box, and The Records

A memorandum for counsel of record in The Regents of the University of California v. Sagitec Solutions, LLC, et al. — prepared voluntarily, without compensation sought, by a UC retiree with 36 years of contemporaneous documentation.
Not for public distribution · Fact-witness memorandum · April 23, 2026

IThe Witness UC Cannot Afford to Put on a Stand

The current UCLA Chief of Police is, on paper, the most qualified chief UCLA has ever hired. He is also the single most dangerous witness The Regents of the University of California could be compelled to produce in Regents v. Sagitec Solutions, LLC.

He is a named respondent in a POST complaint filed by a former UCPD officer whose 30-year records dispute with the University is the subject of this memorandum. The office he holds is the one that took the actions under review. But the argument here is not that a different chief would have acted differently. The argument is that no University of California Chief of Police could.

He is trained to enforce accountability. He runs a department that has never been subject to it. And the records at the heart of The Regents' False Claims Act theory against Sagitec — "UC's pension administration system lost or corrupted records because of Sagitec's defective work" — overlap, directly and materially, with the records this Chief's department is currently refusing to produce.

CREDENTIALS — THE CURRENT UCLA CHIEF OF POLICE
  • UCLA B.A. Political Science, 1992–1996. On campus during the final four years of the UCPD career documented in this memorandum. Graduated the same year Officer 341 was forced out.
  • LAPD 29-year career through command rank. Served during the 2001–2013 federal consent decree (191 reforms under what is now 34 U.S.C. § 12601).
  • Training FBI National Academy. Sherman Block Supervisory Leadership Institute. LAPD Command Development Program.
  • Education Master of Public Administration, University of Southern California.
  • Command North Hollywood Division. Metro Division.
  • UCPD Sworn in as UCLA Chief of Police, October 2025. Fourth UCPD chief in three years.

He is over-qualified for a system that does not use what he was trained to provide. No federal consent decree has ever touched a UC police department. No pattern-or-practice investigation. No external civilian oversight body with subpoena power. The tool he watched work at LAPD — 34 U.S.C. § 12601 — has never been used at any UC campus in the statute's entire existence.

The Trap

If compelled to testify on the University's records practices, its personnel-file custody, its handling of Officer 341's disability reclassification, and the contradictions between UC's written determinations and its own eleven years of conduct, he has exactly two choices:

TWO DOORS · NO THIRD · NEITHER SURVIVES CROSS-EXAMINATION
If he lies under oath: He may keep his position for one news cycle. But every answer is trivially impeachable from a 36-year documented paper trail that already exists — Property Report #0190000204, Grievance AR 91-01-14, the 1991–1992 Skelly hearing record, UC's own 1099-R filings 2009–2024, two CCW permits UCPD itself issued, and the three written 2023–2026 medical-separation determinations signed by his own command staff. Impeachment means perjury exposure under Penal Code § 118. POST-certified peace officers do not survive perjury findings. Career over.
If he tells the truth under oath: UC's False Claims Act theory in v. Sagitec — that the broken system of record is Sagitec's fault — collapses on the witness stand. UC fires him the way it fired Stephen Agostini four days after Agostini told the truth about UCLA's financial records on February 13, 2026. Career over.
The real trap: There is no path where the Chief keeps both his oath and his career. The documentary record already exists. Every lie collides with a document. Every truth collides with The Regents.

UC can fight the subpoena — which looks like concealment and hands Sagitec's counterclaim another paragraph. UC can produce him — and every answer he gives under oath, truthfully, damages The Regents' theory of the case. If he lies to protect The Regents, Sagitec's counsel walks him through the documents one at a time until the record answers for him. The Chief knows this. His training is built on it. His job forbids him from saying so aloud.

The Posture A sworn law-enforcement officer with LAPD consent-decree experience, an MPA from USC, and FBI National Academy credentials, seated under oath, looking at a three-decade paper trail his department is currently refusing to produce. That is not a witness UC General Counsel wants on a calendar.

IIWitness Backup — The Agostini Precedent

The Chief is not hypothetical. The precedent is six weeks old.

On February 13, 2026, UCLA's own Chief Financial Officer, Stephen Agostini, told the Daily Bruin that the University's annual financial reports since 2002 were "erroneous and unaudited," that UCLA carried a $425 million structural deficit, and that a $150 million finance-modernization project had produced "nothing to show for it." He added that he had "rarely seen the kind of financial management flaws and failures" he saw at UCLA.

On February 17, 2026 — four days later — UCLA fired him.

On March 30, 2026, Culver City hired him as Chief Financial Officer. In the hiring announcement, Culver City credited him with closing a $300 million budget gap at UCLA and delivering a balanced budget. The same record that got him fired became the line on his resume that got him his next job.

Why Agostini matters to this memorandum

  • He is a second, already-proven truth-teller with direct institutional knowledge of UCLA's record-keeping failures. UC has already established how it handles insiders who speak on the record.
  • He is still available. He is now employed by a California municipal government and cannot be fired again by UC for testifying.
  • His public statements are already admissible as prior consistent statements against UC's interest. The Daily Bruin record is citable tomorrow.
  • His firing is the proof that UC's institutional response to documented truth-telling is retaliation, not investigation. That pattern bears directly on how the Chief will calculate his testimony.
The Pairing One current UC police chief with institutional custody of 36 years of records. One former UCLA CFO already on the record about UCLA's financial systems being "erroneous and unaudited" since 2002. Together, they establish a pre-existing pattern of broken UC record systems that predates Sagitec's 2013 contract and continues today using software Sagitec did not build.

IIIWhy This Matters to Sagitec's Defense

The Regents' theory, reduced to its essence, is: Sagitec was hired to build a reliable pension-administration system; Sagitec delivered a defective product; UC's record-keeping failures are the result of that defect; triple damages follow under the California False Claims Act.

The record available to this fact witness — developed from UC's own documents and from contemporaneous correspondence spanning 1990 to April 2026 — contradicts that theory in four specific ways:

  • UC's record-keeping was unreliable before Sagitec existed in the University's vendor list. Grievance AR 91-01-14, Property Report #0190000204, the 1991–1992 Skelly hearing, and the 1995–1996 retaliation documentation all predate the 2013 Sagitec contract by two decades. Every grievance Officer 341 filed against the University in those years was won. The University's response to each victory was escalation, not compliance.
  • UC reclassified Officer 341's disability status in 2015 — four years before Sagitec's April 2019 go-live. From 2009 through 2014, UC reported his pension under 1099-R Distribution Code 3 — Disability. In 2015, without a Workers' Compensation Appeals Board hearing and without written authority, UC flipped the code to Code 2 — Early Retirement. UC then reported his income that way for ten consecutive years. Sagitec's system had not yet launched when that reclassification occurred.
  • Three independent UC sources — none of them attributing the failures to Sagitec — have separately acknowledged that the system is broken. Ida Fong (2021, UCLA Benefits processing floor). Stephen Agostini (February 2026, UCLA executive suite). The Regents themselves (December 2025, in their own pleading in this litigation).
  • UC is still producing the same pattern of errors today using software Sagitec never built. On April 16, 2026, a UC benefits representative told Officer 341 that a Medicare option available to every Medicare-eligible American under federal law did not exist for him. That call took place using UC's current retiree-services workflow — not through UCRAYS, not through Sagitec's system.

IVThe Broken System of Record — 1990 to Present

This memorandum does not offer a litigation theory. It offers a timeline. The timeline is documented in 15 chapters of an evidentiary record already drafted and available on request.

Pre-Sagitec (1990–2013)

  • July 1990. Officer 341 issues a parking citation to a Regent's vehicle at a Regents meeting on the UCLA campus. Two commanding officers order the ticket cancelled. Officer 341 refuses, and files Grievance AR 91-01-14 — the first formal grievance in the record. Both commanding officers become central figures in the six-year retaliatory campaign that follows.
  • 1990–1993. UCLA PD internal investigation. Eight tape-recorded conversations placed into evidence under Property Report #0190000204. Findings confirm the whistleblower's allegations. UC's institutional response is escalation.
  • 1991–1992. Every grievance Officer 341 files against the University is won. The Skelly hearing record is preserved.
  • 1996. Officer 341 is forced out of UCPD. The current Chief of Police graduates UCLA the same year.
  • 2001–2003. UC's own claims administrator, Octagon Risk Services, declares Officer 341 permanently and stationarily disabled, retroactive to March 1, 1996. UC reports the pension under 1099-R Code 3 — Disability — from 2009 through 2014.

The Sagitec Window (2013–2019)

  • 2013. UC awards a contract to Sagitec Solutions and Linea Solutions to rebuild the pension administration system.
  • June 1, 2015. A UCRAYS representative calls Officer 341 and tells him he must convert his disability income to a service retirement or he will lose all income. The representation turns out to be false. Without a hearing and without WCAB approval, UC flips his 1099-R from Code 3 — Disability to Code 2 — Early Retirement. UC reports his income that way for ten consecutive years. Sagitec's system does not go live for another four years.
  • April 2019. Sagitec's system launches. Pension payments are delayed. UC later characterizes the launch as "chaos."

Post-Sagitec (2019–Present)

  • 2021. Ida Fong, a UCLA benefits processor, acknowledges on the record that the University's record-keeping loses files. This is on the processing floor, not in a Sagitec system.
  • August 25, 2023 & February 10, 2026. Three command-level officers inside a UC police department — the Captain, the Chief, and the Lieutenant — classify Officer 341's employment, in writing, as "status was designated as medically separated on March 1, 1996," and use that classification to cancel his concealed-carry permit. That written determination directly contradicts the University's own eleven years of treating him as "retired" — including two CCW permits issued by UCLA PD itself.
  • December 2025. The Regents file their pleading in v. Sagitec. The pleading acknowledges systemic errors in UC's pension administration — errors whose scope extends beyond any software Sagitec delivered.
  • February 13, 2026. Stephen Agostini tells the Daily Bruin UCLA's financial reports since 2002 are "erroneous and unaudited." Four days later, he is fired.
  • April 16, 2026. A UC benefits representative tells Officer 341 that a Medicare option available to every Medicare-eligible American does not exist for him. The conversation takes place on current UC infrastructure.

VSchrödinger's Box — The Personnel File

One file resolves the record. UC will not open the box.

The three UC command officers named above have, in writing, classified Officer 341's employment as "medically separated March 1, 1996." That determination contradicts eleven years of UC treating him as "retired," including two concealed-carry permits UCLA PD itself issued. After three formal requests, the officers have withheld his legally protected personnel file — the file Government Code § 3306.5 guarantees every California peace officer the right to inspect.

Opening the file collapses the superposition into exactly one of two measured outcomes. Either outcome costs the University money.

  • State A — "Medically Separated" / Duty Disability Income. The exact status UC itself assigned him in 2001, retroactive to March 1, 1996. UC's own 1099-R records from 2009–2014 confirm it under Distribution Code 3 — Disability. If State A is the observed state, UC owes him hundreds of thousands of dollars in back pay plus the tax consequences of its own misclassification from 2015 forward.
  • State B — "Retired" / Service Retirement. The way UC began treating his record on June 1, 2015 and continued for over a decade. If State B is the observed state, UC unilaterally separated him from an approved workers' compensation case without Workers' Compensation Appeals Board authorization — a violation of Labor Code § 5804, which grants the WCAB continuing jurisdiction over every settlement it approves. The CCW cancellation becomes unlawful on its face.

Either employment classification measurement collapses against the University. So the University refuses to measure. The box is locked. The measurement that would resolve it lives inside records the UCLA Chief of Police's department currently controls.

The Intersection The Regents argue, in v. Sagitec, that their pension system lost or corrupted records because of Sagitec's defective work. But the records at the center of Officer 341's dispute are not pension-calculation records. They are personnel, disability, and peace-officer-status records held in UC systems Sagitec did not build, did not configure, and has no contractual custody of. If The Regents' "broken system of record" theory is true for Sagitec-era data, it is almost certainly true for pre-Sagitec and non-Sagitec data. And if it is true for pre-Sagitec and non-Sagitec data, then the defect is not Sagitec's product. It is UC's institution.

VICompanion Document — The Federal Parallel

The conduct pattern in Regents v. Sagitec is not isolated. The Department of Justice is already prosecuting a parallel fact pattern against UC's insurance counterparties.

Included with this memorandum is a second document — The Epilogue — drawn from a longer evidentiary record and focused on the federal landscape UC is currently operating inside. Three points from that document bear directly on Sagitec's defense posture:

  • United States ex rel. Shea v. eHealth, Inc., et al., No. 1:21-cv-11777-DJC (D. Mass.). On May 1, 2025, the Department of Justice intervened in a qui tam action alleging that three major Medicare Advantage insurers — Humana, Aetna (now CVS Health), and Anthem (now Elevance Health) — paid more than $230 million in illegal kickbacks to three broker organizations in exchange for steering Medicare enrollees. On March 25, 2026, Chief Judge Denise J. Casper denied Elevance Health's motion to dismiss, and the case is proceeding to trial.
  • Anthem is UC's retiree insurer. The same Elevance Health entity the DOJ is prosecuting for Medicare Advantage kickbacks is the entity UC self-funds and contracts with for its retiree health plan. The DOJ complaint alleges the kickback compensation was structured through "Marketing Development Agreements," "Call Development Program Agreements," and "Sponsorship" arrangements whose written terms reference "marketing" and "lead generation" — while the real compensation lived on spreadsheets and phone calls (DOJ Complaint ¶¶416, 585).
  • UC's retiree-benefits funnel runs through the same mechanism. UC transfers Medicare-eligible retirees to Via Benefits / Willis Towers Watson via contracts whose precise compensation structure The Regents have not disclosed publicly. Mr. Harold's experience — documented in the Epilogue — describes identical operational mechanics: aggressive pre-Medicare outreach, representatives who present as UC personnel, and a unidirectional funnel whose financial incentive runs only one way.

The relevance to Sagitec is not that Sagitec has exposure in Shea. Sagitec has no exposure in Shea. The relevance is that UC is currently suing its pension-system vendor for alleged fraud while itself operating inside a retiree-benefits funnel that the Department of Justice is, as of March 25, 2026, allowing to proceed to trial against UC's own insurance counterparty. The "broken system of record" that The Regents attribute to Sagitec is, in the Epilogue's documented view, a feature of UC's retiree-administration infrastructure as a whole — not a defect introduced by one vendor.

Why This Helps Sagitec If The Regents must explain, during discovery, why their pension vendor is answerable in False Claims Act damages while their insurance carrier — with an ongoing federal prosecution and a denied motion to dismiss — is not even named in a Regents action, the credibility of The Regents' theory of systemic vendor fraud takes a measurable hit. Counsel may also note that Mr. Harold's DMHC complaint (California Department of Managed Health Care, Case #1409109) is separately preserving a record of UC's current retiree-benefits conduct, independent of this litigation.
